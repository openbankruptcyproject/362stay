How Long Does the Automatic Stay Last?

Duration of the stay for first-time and repeat filers, and how the court can lift it early

Home › Duration

First-Time Filers: Stay Lasts the Entire Case

If you have not had a bankruptcy case dismissed within the past year, the automatic stay under 11 U.S.C. Section 362(a) remains in effect for the duration of your bankruptcy case. That means:

  • Chapter 7: The stay lasts until discharge (typically 3-4 months) or until the case is dismissed or closed
  • Chapter 13: The stay lasts for the entire plan period (3-5 years), until discharge, dismissal, or closing
  • Chapter 11: The stay lasts until the plan is confirmed and the case is closed or converted

The stay terminates automatically with respect to the debtor's property when the case is closed, dismissed, or the debtor receives a discharge. It terminates with respect to estate property when that property is no longer part of the estate.

Repeat Filers: Reduced or No Stay

One dismissal in the past year -- 362(c)(3)

If you had a bankruptcy case pending within the preceding one year that was dismissed, the automatic stay in your new case terminates after 30 days unless you file a motion to extend it. The court will extend the stay only if you demonstrate that the new case was filed in good faith.

The 30-day deadline is strict. If you miss it, the stay expires and creditors can resume collection. File the extension motion immediately upon filing the new case.

Two or more dismissals in the past year -- 362(c)(4)

If you had two or more cases pending within the preceding year that were dismissed, the automatic stay does not go into effect at all when you file a new case. You must file a motion asking the court to impose the stay, and prove that the new filing is in good faith.

Good faith factors: Courts consider whether you are addressing the issues that led to prior dismissals, whether your financial circumstances have changed, whether you are likely to complete the new case, and whether the filing appears to be a tactic to delay creditors.

When the Court Can Lift the Stay Early

Even in a first-time filing, creditors can file a motion for relief from stay under Section 362(d). The court may grant relief if:

  • Cause exists -- including lack of adequate protection of the creditor's interest in property (Section 362(d)(1))
  • No equity + not necessary for reorganization -- the debtor has no equity in the property and it is not necessary for an effective reorganization (Section 362(d)(2))
  • Single asset real estate -- special rules under Section 362(d)(3)

If the court grants relief from stay, the stay is lifted only as to that specific creditor and that specific property. The stay remains in effect against all other creditors.
