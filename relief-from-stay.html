Motions for Relief from Stay

When creditors ask the bankruptcy court to lift the automatic stay so they can proceed with foreclosure, repossession, or other collection.

Home›Relief from Stay

What Is a Motion for Relief from Stay?

A motion for relief from stay (sometimes called a "lift stay motion" or "MFR") is a request by a creditor asking the bankruptcy court to modify, condition, or terminate the automatic stay so the creditor can exercise its rights outside of bankruptcy. This is the creditor's primary tool for getting around the stay without violating it.

The legal authority is 11 U.S.C. Section 362(d), which provides four grounds for relief. The motion is filed in the bankruptcy court, the debtor has the right to respond and be heard, and the court must act within strict time limits.

Grounds for Relief

Section 362(d) provides four separate grounds. A creditor needs to establish only one.

362(d)(1): For Cause, Including Lack of Adequate Protection

The most common ground. "Cause" is not defined in the statute and is determined on a case-by-case basis. The most frequently litigated form of cause is lack of adequate protection - when the creditor's interest in collateral is declining in value and the debtor is not making payments or otherwise protecting the creditor's position. For example, if a homeowner has stopped making mortgage payments and the property is declining in value, the lender can argue that its security interest is not adequately protected.

Other forms of "cause" include: the debtor has no intention of reorganizing, the property is not insured, or the debtor is wasting the collateral.

362(d)(2): No Equity and Not Necessary for Reorganization

Relief is granted if the debtor has no equity in the property AND the property is not necessary for an effective reorganization. Both elements must be met. This ground is commonly used for underwater properties - where the debt exceeds the value of the collateral. In Chapter 7 cases, property is rarely "necessary for reorganization," making this ground easier to establish. In Chapter 13 cases, the debtor can argue that a vehicle or home is necessary for the reorganization plan.

362(d)(3): Single Asset Real Estate

For "single asset real estate" cases (a debtor whose primary asset is a single piece of real property generating income), the creditor can get relief if the debtor has not filed a reorganization plan within 90 days of the order for relief or started making interest payments to the creditor. This provision prevents investors from using bankruptcy to delay foreclosure on rental or commercial property without a genuine reorganization effort.

362(d)(4): Scheme to Delay, Hinder, or Defraud

Relief is granted if the court finds that the filing is part of a scheme to delay, hinder, or defraud creditors involving either a transfer of property without consent of the secured creditor or multiple bankruptcy filings affecting the same property. If relief is granted under this subsection, it can be made in rem - meaning it attaches to the property itself and prevents future filings from staying enforcement against that property for two years.

Timeline and Procedure

Motion Filed

The creditor files the motion and serves it on the debtor, debtor's attorney, the trustee, and any committee. A filing fee is required (currently $181 in most districts).

30 Days: Preliminary Hearing or Final Hearing

Under Section 362(e)(1), the court must commence a final hearing within 30 days of the motion being filed. If the final hearing is not commenced within 30 days, the stay is automatically terminated against the movant. The court can continue the preliminary hearing for up to 30 additional days for cause, but only if the stay remains in effect during the continuance.

Response Deadline

The debtor typically has 14 days to respond (check local rules - some districts allow more or less time). A failure to respond may result in the motion being granted by default.

Hearing and Order

If the debtor opposes, the court holds a hearing. The burden of proof on equity issues falls on the debtor. The burden on all other issues falls on the creditor. The court may grant, deny, or condition relief (e.g., granting relief only if the debtor fails to make adequate protection payments going forward).

Do not ignore a motion for relief from stay. If you fail to respond and appear, the court may grant the motion by default. Once the stay is lifted, the creditor can proceed immediately with foreclosure, repossession, or other collection - and you lose the protection that makes bankruptcy effective.

How to Oppose a Motion for Relief from Stay

If you receive a motion for relief from stay, you need to act quickly. Here are the key strategies for opposing:

Show Adequate Protection

If the creditor argues lack of adequate protection, demonstrate that you are making payments (or propose to start), the property is insured, and the creditor's equity cushion is sufficient. Adequate protection can take the form of periodic cash payments, an additional lien on other property, or other relief that gives the creditor the "indubitable equivalent" of its interest.

Demonstrate Equity

If the creditor argues no equity under 362(d)(2), present evidence that the property is worth more than the debt - through appraisals, comparable sales, or tax assessments. Even a small amount of equity can defeat this prong.

Prove Necessity for Reorganization

In Chapter 13 cases, argue that the property is necessary for your reorganization - a vehicle you need for work, a home that provides housing, or business equipment needed to generate income for plan payments. You must have a viable plan that proposes to cure the arrearage and maintain ongoing payments.

Propose Conditions

Courts often grant conditional relief rather than outright termination. You can propose conditions: "Deny the motion so long as the debtor makes monthly adequate protection payments of $X beginning on [date]." This approach keeps the stay in place while protecting the creditor's interest going forward.

Effect of Relief from Stay

When the court grants relief from stay, the creditor is authorized to proceed with its state-law remedies as if the bankruptcy had not been filed - but only with respect to the specific property or action covered by the order. The stay remains in effect as to all other creditors and all other property.

Relief from stay does not discharge the debt, forgive the obligation, or affect the debtor's right to object to the creditor's claim in the bankruptcy case. It simply removes the stay's injunction with respect to that specific creditor and that specific collateral.

If relief is granted and the creditor forecloses or repossesses, the debtor may still have a claim for any deficiency or surplus, depending on state law and the terms of the bankruptcy plan.

Foundational Caselaw

The cases below define the doctrine on this site:

  • Citizens Bank of Maryland v. Strumpf, 516 U.S. 16 (1995) - administrative freeze of a debtor's bank account does not violate Section 362(a)(3) so long as the bank does not unilaterally deduct funds; defines what does NOT constitute "exercise control over property of the estate."
  • Mwangi v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (In re Mwangi), 432 B.R. 812 (9th Cir. BAP 2010) - bank that froze debtor's account without releasing exempt funds promptly violated the automatic stay; defines what DOES constitute a stay violation.
  • Taggart v. Lorenzen, 587 U.S. 554 (2019) - civil-contempt standard for discharge-injunction and stay violations: contempt is appropriate only when there is no fair ground of doubt as to whether the order barred the creditor's conduct.
