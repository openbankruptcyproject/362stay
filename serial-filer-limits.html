Automatic Stay Limits for Repeat Filers

Sections 362(c)(3) and 362(c)(4) restrict or eliminate automatic stay protection when a debtor has had recent dismissed cases.

Home›Repeat Filer Limits

Why Repeat Filers Get Less Protection

Before 2005, every bankruptcy filing triggered a full automatic stay regardless of how many prior cases the debtor had filed. Congress changed this with the Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention and Consumer Protection Act (BAPCPA), adding Sections 362(c)(3) and 362(c)(4) to address serial filings -- cases filed primarily to invoke the stay without intending to complete the bankruptcy process.

The concern was that some debtors (or their attorneys) were filing bankruptcy petitions solely to stop foreclosure sales or repossessions, allowing the case to be dismissed, and then refiling repeatedly. Each filing would trigger a new stay, potentially delaying creditors for months or years without any real reorganization effort.

The result is a two-tier system based on how many dismissed cases the debtor has had in the preceding year.

Section 362(c)(3)

Trigger 1 prior case dismissed in past year Stay Duration 30 days (then expires) Remedy Motion to extend stay

Section 362(c)(4)

Trigger 2+ prior cases dismissed in past year Stay Duration No stay at all Remedy Motion to impose stay

Section 362(c)(3): The 30-Day Stay

If a debtor had one prior bankruptcy case that was pending within the preceding one-year period and that case was dismissed, the automatic stay in the new case terminates after 30 days with respect to the debtor (and in some courts, with respect to property of the estate) unless the court extends it.

What "Pending Within the Preceding One-Year Period" Means

The one-year lookback is measured from the new filing date. The prior case must have been pending (open) at any point during that year and then dismissed. It does not matter when the prior case was originally filed -- only that it was pending and dismissed within the year before the new filing.

How to Extend the Stay

The debtor must file a motion to extend the stay before the 30 days expire. The motion must be set for hearing within the 30-day period. The debtor must demonstrate by clear and convincing evidence that the new case was filed in good faith.

There is a rebuttable presumption that the case was NOT filed in good faith if:

  • The prior case was dismissed after the debtor failed to file required documents (schedules, plan, etc.)
  • The prior case was dismissed after the debtor failed to perform the terms of a confirmed plan
  • There has been no substantial change in the debtor's financial or personal circumstances since the prior dismissal

To overcome this presumption, the debtor typically must show that the reason for the prior dismissal has been resolved -- for example, a new source of income, resolution of a dispute that disrupted plan payments, or a changed financial situation that makes the new case viable.

Critical timing: The motion must be filed and heard within 30 days of the petition date. If the debtor misses this window, the stay terminates by operation of law. Some courts will not grant extensions after the 30 days have passed. File the motion immediately upon filing the new case.

Section 362(c)(4): No Stay at All

If the debtor had two or more prior cases that were pending within the preceding one-year period and were dismissed, the new filing does not trigger any automatic stay. Creditors may proceed with foreclosure, repossession, and collection actions as if no bankruptcy was filed.

How to Get the Stay Imposed

The debtor must file a motion asking the court to impose the stay. The court may impose the stay only if the debtor demonstrates by clear and convincing evidence that the new case was filed in good faith as to the creditors to be stayed. The same presumption of bad faith applies as under Section 362(c)(3), and it is even harder to overcome because the debtor has a pattern of multiple dismissed cases.

Until the court grants the motion, there is no stay. The debtor has no automatic protection. This means that in the days or weeks between filing and the hearing on the motion, creditors are free to act. For this reason, debtors in this situation should file the motion to impose the stay simultaneously with the petition and request an emergency or expedited hearing.

Circuit Split: Does 362(c)(3) Terminate the Stay as to Estate Property?

There is a significant circuit split on whether Section 362(c)(3) terminates the stay only as to the debtor personally, or also as to property of the bankruptcy estate. The statute says the stay "shall terminate with respect to the debtor" after 30 days. Some courts read this literally -- the stay terminates only as to the debtor, but remains in effect as to property of the estate. Other courts interpret the provision broadly to terminate the stay as to both the debtor and estate property.

This distinction matters enormously for homeowners. If the stay remains in effect as to property of the estate (the house), the lender still cannot foreclose even after 30 days. If the stay terminates as to everything, the lender can proceed. Check the law in your circuit before relying on either interpretation.

Interaction with Other Filing Bars

Repeat filers face multiple overlapping restrictions beyond the stay limitations:

  • Section 1328(f) -- Bars discharge (not just the stay) for repeat Chapter 13 filers within 2 or 4 years of a prior filing, depending on the chapter of the prior case
  • Section 109(g) -- Bars filing entirely for 180 days after a voluntary dismissal following a motion for relief from stay, or after a dismissal for willful failure to comply with court orders
  • Section 727(a)(8)/(a)(9) -- Bars Chapter 7 discharge for 8 years after a prior Chapter 7 filing or 6 years after a prior Chapter 13 filing

A debtor who refiles within the Section 362(c)(3) or (c)(4) window may also face a discharge bar that makes the new filing pointless even if the stay is extended or imposed. The 1328(f) Discharge Screener can help determine whether a prior filing creates a discharge bar.
