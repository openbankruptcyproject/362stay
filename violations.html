Creditor Violated the Automatic Stay?

What to do when a creditor ignores the bankruptcy filing and how to recover damages under 11 U.S.C. Section 362(k).

Home›Violations

Understanding Stay Violations

The automatic stay is not a suggestion - it is a federal court order that takes effect the instant a bankruptcy petition is filed. Any action by a creditor to collect a pre-petition debt, enforce a lien, or repossess property after the filing violates the stay and may be void or voidable.

Courts take stay violations seriously because the stay is fundamental to the bankruptcy process. Without it, the orderly administration of the estate - and the debtor's ability to reorganize or receive a fresh start - would be impossible.

Common Stay Violations

  • Continuing collection calls or sending demand letters after receiving notice of the filing
  • Repossessing a vehicle after the petition date
  • Proceeding with foreclosure when the lender had notice of the bankruptcy
  • Garnishing wages after the filing, or refusing to return garnished funds
  • Filing or continuing a lawsuit against the debtor for a pre-petition debt
  • Sending account statements with collection language or threats
  • Reporting the debt as delinquent to credit bureaus after the filing (in some circuits)
  • Offsetting bank account funds against a pre-petition loan balance
  • Refusing to reconnect utilities despite the stay and adequate assurance of payment

Section 362(k): Your Right to Damages

11 U.S.C. Section 362(k)(1) provides that an individual injured by any willful violation of the stay shall recover actual damages, including costs and attorney fees, and in appropriate circumstances, may recover punitive damages.

What Counts as "Willful"?

A violation is "willful" if the creditor knew of the stay and intentionally took the action that violated it. The creditor does not need to have intended to violate the law - only to have intended the act itself. In most circuits, if a creditor knew about the bankruptcy filing and still repossessed the vehicle, that is willful. The creditor's good faith belief that the action was permissible is generally not a defense.

Types of Damages Available

  • Actual damages - Out-of-pocket costs caused by the violation (towing fees, rental car costs, lost wages from missing work, bank fees, etc.)
  • Emotional distress damages - Available in many circuits when the violation causes significant distress, though some circuits require evidence beyond mere annoyance
  • Attorney fees and costs - The debtor can recover the cost of bringing the motion to enforce the stay
  • Punitive damages - Available when the creditor's conduct is egregious, in bad faith, or shows a pattern of disregard for the stay. Courts have awarded punitive damages ranging from a few thousand dollars to six figures in extreme cases

Important: Section 362(k) applies only to "individuals." Courts are split on whether business entities (corporations, LLCs) can recover damages under this section. Some courts allow business debtors to seek sanctions under Section 105(a) instead.

How to Document a Stay Violation

Strong documentation is critical to proving a stay violation and recovering damages. Start building your evidence the moment you become aware of the violation.

1. Preserve All Communications

Save every letter, email, voicemail, and text message from the creditor. Screenshot caller ID logs. If a debt collector calls, note the date, time, caller's name, and what was said. Do not delete voicemails - forward them to email if possible.

2. Document the Timeline

Create a written timeline showing: (1) when the bankruptcy was filed, (2) when the creditor received notice, (3) when each violating action occurred, and (4) what harm resulted. The time stamp on the bankruptcy petition is the legal start of the stay.

3. Confirm Notice Was Received

The bankruptcy court sends notice to all listed creditors, but there can be a delay of days or weeks. If your attorney (or you) sent separate notice to the creditor - by fax, email, or certified mail - retain that proof. Direct notice strengthens the "willful" element because the creditor cannot claim ignorance.

4. Calculate Your Damages

Gather receipts, bank statements, pay stubs, and any other evidence of financial harm. If you missed work because your car was repossessed, document the days missed and wages lost. If you paid for a rental car, keep the receipt. If the violation caused emotional distress, document the impact - medical records, prescriptions, or a written description of how the violation affected your daily life.

5. File a Motion for Contempt

The motion is filed in the bankruptcy court where your case is pending. It should describe the stay, the violation, the creditor's knowledge of the filing, and the damages suffered. Many districts have local forms. If you have an attorney, they will handle this. If you are pro se, check your court's website for motion templates and local rules on hearing procedures.

What Happens After You File the Motion

The court will typically set a hearing. The creditor must respond to the motion and may argue that the violation was not willful, that they had no notice, or that the stay did not apply (e.g., because an exception under Section 362(b) applied).

If the court finds a willful violation, it will order the creditor to take corrective action - returning property, reversing garnishments, ceasing collection activity - and award damages. The court may also refer the matter for additional sanctions under Section 105(a) or even report the conduct to regulatory authorities.

Some creditors settle before the hearing once they realize the violation is documented. The possibility of punitive damages and attorney fee-shifting creates a strong incentive for creditors to resolve violations quickly.

Void vs. Voidable Actions

Courts disagree on whether actions taken in violation of the stay are automatically void (as if they never happened) or merely voidable (valid until the court says otherwise). The majority rule in most circuits is that stay violations are void ab initio, meaning the action has no legal effect from the moment it occurs. Under this rule, a foreclosure sale conducted in violation of the stay conveys no title, regardless of whether the buyer or the lender knew about the bankruptcy.

The minority rule treats violations as voidable, giving the court discretion to validate the action in appropriate circumstances. This distinction matters most in real property transactions where innocent third-party buyers may be involved.

Foundational Caselaw

The cases below define the doctrine on this site:

  • Citizens Bank of Maryland v. Strumpf, 516 U.S. 16 (1995) - administrative freeze of a debtor's bank account does not violate Section 362(a)(3) so long as the bank does not unilaterally deduct funds; defines what does NOT constitute "exercise control over property of the estate."
  • Mwangi v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (In re Mwangi), 432 B.R. 812 (9th Cir. BAP 2010) - bank that froze debtor's account without releasing exempt funds promptly violated the automatic stay; defines what DOES constitute a stay violation.
  • Taggart v. Lorenzen, 587 U.S. 554 (2019) - civil-contempt standard for discharge-injunction and stay violations: contempt is appropriate only when there is no fair ground of doubt as to whether the order barred the creditor's conduct.
