What the Automatic Stay Stops

A complete breakdown of what creditors can and cannot do once you file for bankruptcy under 11 U.S.C. Section 362.

Home›What It Stops

Actions the Automatic Stay Stops

Section 362(a) creates a broad injunction that halts nearly all creditor activity the moment a bankruptcy petition is filed. Below is a practical guide to the most common actions that are stayed, organized by category.

Foreclosure

All mortgage foreclosure proceedings are halted, whether judicial or non-judicial. A scheduled foreclosure sale must be canceled or postponed. Even if the sale was hours away at the time of filing, it cannot proceed. If a foreclosure sale occurs after the petition is filed but before the creditor learns of the filing, courts generally treat the sale as void. The debtor may need to file the case with enough lead time for the court to notify the creditor - but the legal effect is retroactive to the moment of filing. See Section 362(a)(1), (a)(3), (a)(4).

Vehicle Repossession

Auto lenders cannot repossess vehicles after the petition is filed. If a vehicle is repossessed before filing, the debtor may be able to compel turnover of the vehicle under Section 542, depending on the timing and jurisdiction. If a creditor repossesses a vehicle after the filing, that is a clear stay violation under Section 362(k), and the creditor must return the vehicle and may be liable for damages. This applies to all secured personal property - not just vehicles.

Wage Garnishment

All wage garnishments for pre-petition debts must stop. The debtor or debtor's attorney should notify the employer and the garnishing creditor of the bankruptcy filing. Any wages garnished after the petition date must be returned. State-law garnishment proceedings are stayed under Section 362(a)(1) and (a)(2). Note: garnishments for domestic support obligations (child support, alimony) are NOT stayed. See exceptions.

Lawsuits Against the Debtor

All pending civil lawsuits against the debtor are frozen. No new lawsuits can be filed. This includes breach of contract claims, personal injury lawsuits (with some exceptions), collection suits, and deficiency actions. The plaintiff in a pending lawsuit must be notified and the case is typically stayed by filing a "Suggestion of Bankruptcy" in the state or federal court where the lawsuit is pending. The lawsuit remains frozen until the bankruptcy court lifts the stay or the case closes.

Collection Calls and Letters

All collection activity must cease. This includes phone calls, letters, emails, text messages, and social media contacts. Debt collectors who continue calling after receiving notice of the filing violate both the automatic stay and potentially the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act. The debtor does not need to individually notify every creditor - the filing itself creates the stay - but providing written notice removes any defense that the violation was not "willful."

Utility Disconnections

Under Section 366, utility companies (electric, gas, water, phone) cannot disconnect service for 20 days after the petition is filed based on pre-petition debt. Within that 20-day period, the debtor must provide adequate assurance of future payment - typically a deposit equal to one month's estimated service. If the debtor provides adequate assurance, the utility cannot disconnect for the duration of the case. Post-petition utility bills must be paid as they come due.

Bank Account Levies and Freezes

Creditors cannot levy or freeze bank accounts after the petition is filed. If a creditor has already obtained a court order to seize funds but has not yet executed the levy, the stay prevents execution. If funds are frozen or seized after the filing, the creditor must release them. Banks sometimes freeze accounts upon learning of a bankruptcy filing to determine whether funds are property of the estate - this is distinct from a creditor levy and is generally permissible for a brief period.

Tax Collection

The IRS and state tax authorities cannot proceed with liens, levies, or seizures for pre-petition tax debts. Tax assessments can continue (this is an exception under Section 362(b)(9)), but active collection is stayed. The stay does not prevent the filing of a tax return or the issuance of a tax refund, but it does prevent the IRS from offsetting a refund against pre-petition debt in some circumstances. Tax liens that attached before the filing remain in place but cannot be enforced.

Eviction (Limited)

Eviction proceedings are stayed by the filing, but Section 362(b)(22) creates a significant exception: if the landlord obtained a judgment for possession before the petition was filed, the eviction may proceed despite the stay. The debtor can delay by filing a certification (and deposit) under Section 362(l) claiming the right to cure under applicable state law, but this provides only a 30-day window. In practice, the automatic stay provides much less protection for renters facing eviction than for homeowners facing foreclosure.

What the Stay Does NOT Stop

The following actions are specifically exempted from the automatic stay under Section 362(b). Creditors or other parties can continue these actions even while the bankruptcy case is pending.

Criminal Proceedings

The government can prosecute criminal cases regardless of a bankruptcy filing. This includes criminal fraud charges, DUI proceedings, and any other criminal prosecution. Section 362(b)(1).

Domestic Support Obligations

Collection of child support and alimony is not stayed. This includes wage withholding orders for support, contempt proceedings for unpaid support, and paternity and support establishment actions. Section 362(b)(2).

Certain Tax Proceedings

Tax audits, tax assessments, tax demands, and issuance of deficiency notices may continue. Active collection (levies, seizures) is stayed, but the assessment process is not. Section 362(b)(9).

Government Regulatory and Police Power

Government agencies can continue enforcing regulatory actions related to public health, safety, and welfare. This includes environmental enforcement, building code violations, and professional license proceedings. Section 362(b)(4).

Prior Eviction Judgments

If a landlord already has a judgment for possession before the filing, eviction can proceed under Section 362(b)(22). The debtor has a narrow cure right under Section 362(l) in some states, but it is limited to 30 days.

Foundational Caselaw

The cases below define the doctrine on this site:

  • Citizens Bank of Maryland v. Strumpf, 516 U.S. 16 (1995) - administrative freeze of a debtor's bank account does not violate Section 362(a)(3) so long as the bank does not unilaterally deduct funds; defines what does NOT constitute "exercise control over property of the estate."
  • Mwangi v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (In re Mwangi), 432 B.R. 812 (9th Cir. BAP 2010) - bank that froze debtor's account without releasing exempt funds promptly violated the automatic stay; defines what DOES constitute a stay violation.
  • Taggart v. Lorenzen, 587 U.S. 554 (2019) - civil-contempt standard for discharge-injunction and stay violations: contempt is appropriate only when there is no fair ground of doubt as to whether the order barred the creditor's conduct.
